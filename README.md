## Introduction
Since the emergence of LLM platforms such as ChatGPT, Claude, DeepSeek, Perplexity, Gemini, and others, we (Editorial Team) at the journal have been asking ourselves: How does this affect our workflow, our ethical approach, and our originality? Also, how is the privacy of authors and their data protected in these tools? These are difficult questions to answer. 

This 2026, as a university-based journal, we have been consulting and checking with publishers such as Wiley, Elsevier, and Springer Nature in order to reach a consensus and establish common guidelines for the use of AI and generative artificial intelligence (AI) in our journal, IJANR. This will be an ongoing review process and will include recommendations and protocols for authors, reviewers, associate editors, technical editors, managing editors, and the editor-in-chief.

## Definitions

Generative AI:  Group of AI algorithms and models that are capable of producing **new content**, including texts, images, videos and, problem-solving strategies, with human-like creativity and adaptability (He et al., 2025)

Prompt engineering: Process of instructing AI or, more precisely, it is the skill and practice of writing inputs (“prompts”) for generative models (Oppenlaender et al., 2023)

Confabulated references: fake citations that often mimic real authors and journals, making detection difficult and contaminating the peer-review ecosystem (Fiorillo 2026)

## Guidelines for authors

1. **Acceptable uses of AI**: improving writing, providing statistical support for data analysis, and offering methodological support, provided that the final content reflects human authorship and expertise.
2. **The use of AI must be disclosed**: specifying the tool, its purpose, and how the generated content was reviewed and validated, except when it is limited to basic language corrections. Authors must declare this use in the Methods section.
3. **We do not allow AI-generated artwork**: There are many scientific illustrators who can produce high-quality content without copyright issues, enriching the scientific ecosystem with new material created by humans.
4. **Coding using AI**: While AI coding tools can accelerate software development, their use in scientific computing raises concerns about code quality and validity. We promote coding development based on problem understanding, context management, testing, and validation. Sharing repositories, code, and data along with your manuscript can ensure reliable and reproducible science (Bridgefort et al., 2025)
5. **Confabulated references are misconduct**: Introducing fake references into a manuscript constitutes a clear academic misconduct due to it violates basic principles of honesty and accuracy in research (Fiorillo 2026)

## Guidelines for reviewers

Manuscripts sent for peer-review are **confidential**. Reviewers must not **upload** manuscripts or any parts of them to AI Tools, as this may violate author confidentiality, proprietary rights, and data privacy. However, [offline local LLMs](https://github.com/rafska/awesome-local-llm) can be used as aid in the review process. Remember that all review comments must reflect the judgement and expertise of the reviewer. We encourage authors not to submit AI-generated outputs directly without refining them with their own expertise, nor to provide extensive comments on formatting or writing style (that is the journal’s responsibility). We need your **scientific expertise**, it is the most valuable asset to the journal.

## Guidelines for associate editors

Similar to reviewers, associate editors must not **upload** manuscripts or any parts of them to AI Tools, as this may violate author confidentiality, proprietary rights, and data privacy. However, [offline local LLMs](https://github.com/rafska/awesome-local-llm) can be used as aid in the review process but **never be involved in editorial decisions**. Acceptable examples include checking the date of citations (e.g., the year per section), verifying the scope, checking abbreviations, etc. We need your eye overseeing the peer-review process, it is the most valuable asset to the journal.

## Guidelines and commitments as journal

The fact that AI has become an integral part of the academic publishing process cannot be ignored. With the rise of automated tools for plagiarism detection, peer review assistance, and workflow optimization, the editorial process is no stranger to this transformation. However, the ethical challenges and potential biases posed by the adoption of AI raise critical questions about fairness and accountability.

As IJANR, we commit to ensuring a peer-review process where:

1. **AI does not replace human responsibility**: reviewers and editors remain fully responsible for the accuracy, quality, and ethical standards of the content.
2. **Data protection and copyright are central**: authors must ensure that AI tools do not claim ownership of the content or use it for training without consent. Offline AI tools will be used and promoted. 
3. **Improving editorial efficiency through the responsible use of AI**: In 2026, we will evaluate the use of AI for automatic formatting checks, as well as for the thematic classification and tagging of manuscripts, to expedite the assignment of associate editors. **AI will never be used for editorial decision-making**.
4. **Protect the integrity of the scientific record**:  Applying preventive measures and calling to action against misconduct related to generative-AI content.
